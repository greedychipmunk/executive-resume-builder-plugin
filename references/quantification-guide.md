# Quantification Guide

The single biggest differentiator between an average resume and a strong one is
**quantified impact**. Numbers make accomplishments credible, comparable, and
memorable. This guide explains how to find metrics and frame them — without ever
fabricating them.

## The impact formula

Every accomplishment bullet should answer three things:

```
[Action verb] + [what you did] + [quantified result / scale / context]
```

- **Action verb** — see `action-verbs.md`.
- **What you did** — the specific work, concisely.
- **Result** — the measurable outcome, *or* the scale/context if a clean metric
  doesn't exist (team size, budget, # of users, geographic scope, timeline).

Lead with the result when it's impressive: *"Grew ARR 40% to $25M by …"*.

## Where to find numbers (interview the candidate)

Most people undercount their impact. Prompt for:

- **Money** — revenue, ARR/MRR, cost savings, budget owned, P&L, fundraising,
  deal sizes, margin change.
- **People** — team size, # of direct/indirect reports, hires, cross-functional
  partners, # of stakeholders/executives influenced.
- **Scale** — users/customers, transactions, requests/sec, data volume, # of
  markets/countries, # of products/SKUs, lines of business.
- **Time** — cycle-time reduction, time-to-market, time saved per week, how fast a
  result was achieved ("in the first 90 days").
- **Percentages & multiples** — growth %, efficiency gains, error-rate reduction,
  NPS/CSAT change, "2x", "10x".
- **Rank / comparison** — "top 5% of region", "#1 of 14 teams", "first ever".
- **Frequency / consistency** — "for 6 consecutive quarters", "across 3 reorgs".

## When the exact number isn't known

Never invent figures. Instead:

1. Help the user **estimate honestly** with a defensible basis ("about how many
   users? a rough % change?"). A reasonable, true estimate beats a vague claim.
2. Use **range or approximation** language: "~30%", "$2–3M", "double-digit growth".
3. If still unknown, insert a placeholder for the user to fill:
   `[QUANTIFY: e.g., 30% / $2M / 12 reports]`.
4. Failing all metrics, quantify **scale/context** instead of outcome (team size,
   budget, scope) — still far stronger than an unquantified duty.

## Framing techniques

- **Result-first** when the number is the headline: *"Cut churn 22% by redesigning
  onboarding."*
- **Baseline → outcome** to show the delta: *"Grew NPS from 31 to 58 in 18 months."*
- **Absolute + relative** for credibility: *"Reduced infra cost 30% ($480K/yr)."*
- **Normalize huge or tiny numbers** so they land: per-day, per-customer, vs. peers.
- **Attribute honestly** — "contributed to" vs. "drove" vs. "owned". Don't claim
  sole credit for team outcomes; do claim your specific leverage.

## Before / after

| Unquantified | Quantified |
|--------------|------------|
| Improved customer satisfaction | Raised CSAT from 78% to 91% across 40K monthly tickets |
| Managed a large budget | Owned a $14M annual P&L across 3 product lines |
| Led a successful product launch | Launched 2 products generating $6M in first-year revenue |
| Sped up the release process | Cut release cycle from 3 weeks to 2 days via CI/CD overhaul |
| Grew the team | Scaled the org from 8 to 45 across 4 countries in 18 months |

## Quality checks

- Does each accomplishment bullet contain a number *or* concrete scale? Aim for the
  large majority to be quantified (the `ats_score.py` impact dimension measures this).
- Are the numbers **verifiable and true**? If asked in an interview, can the
  candidate explain how they got there?
- Are units and timeframes clear? "increased sales" is weak; "increased sales 35%
  YoY" is strong.
- Avoid number soup — one or two strong figures per bullet, not five.
